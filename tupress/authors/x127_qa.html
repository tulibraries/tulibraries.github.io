Take Out
Queer Writing from Asian Pacific America
edited by Quang Bao and Hanya Yanagihara with Timothy Liu

Quang Bao, co-editor of Take Out, speaks out on the features chosen for his new anthology and the diversity and power seen in literary works and visual art from Queer Asian America.

Q: Why did you include poetry, prose, drama, and visual art all into one anthology?
A: We wanted to be open to whatever work artists wanted to send in. Writers are sometimes also visual artists, poets, and performers all in one. Part of the joy of an anthology is its diversity and the collection of different forms and genres.

Q: Was there a specific criteria-other than being queer and Asian-that determined inclusion in TAKE OUT?
A: As we mentioned in the introduction, we had a very laissez-faire policy towards the submissions. We didn't want to impose any limitations, especially on themes. We received-I don't know-several hundred submissions, and then we set out to commission work from writers.

Q: You said in your introduction that you wanted to feature as many "unpublished" voices as possibly, yet TAKE OUT also includes work by established writers, such as Mei Ng (Eating Chinese Food Naked). What balance were you looking to achieve?
A: We drew up a list of writers we admired and wanted to include, not knowing exactly what would come to us from the call for submissions, which went out nationally. I think editors and readers are always interested in new voices. The work by established authors running along side the work from emerging writers augur well for the future of gay/lesbian Asian American literature.

Q: The perspectives in TAKE OUT are quite diverse. Does this book try to include all of the voices of queer Asian America, or is it just representational of new experiences?
A: It shows off the range and diversity of talent and experience. There are people we would have liked to include but quite honestly I think we rounded up a very good group. You might see differences too between what women write about and what men write about or differences in writing from either coasts.

Q: It seems every country from India to Japan is in TAKE OUT. Are there are any cultures that are missing, or you hoped to include, but did not get any submissions?
A: Oh, we didn't pay attention to darting every country on the board. You can't force writers into a book—and whose to say that a writer from Bangladesh is representative of anything Bangladeshi—and you certainly shouldn't have that mindset just because you somehow feel that people not represented will be offended. A book, even one of this size, cannot be all things to all people.

Q: You say in your introduction that "the best anthology is one that makes you reconsider the genre and audience it claims to represent." How does TAKE OUT attempt to accomplish this?
A: The inclusion of literary quality writing that focuses on innumerable subjects in a fresh light. How open a reader's mind becomes depends on where he or she starts out. Above all politics, the book will be a wild ride for readers.
