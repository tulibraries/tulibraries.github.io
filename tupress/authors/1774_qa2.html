City of Sisterly and Brotherly Loves
Lesbian and Gay Philadelphia, 1945-1972
Marc Stein

Marc Stein talks about his view of Philadelphia's gay and lesbian history.

By J. Cooper Robb for the Philadelphia Weekly, 6 October 2004

Marc Stein, a professor of history at Toronto's York University and a former activist with ACT-UP and Queer Nation, is in town this week to read from his book City of Sisterly and Brotherly Loves: Lesbian and Gay Philadelphia, 1945-1972, now out in paperback from Temple University Press.

Q: Your book is scrupulously researched. How many docu-ments did you use and how many people did you speak with?
A: "I interviewed 45 people. I probably looked at thousands of both published and unpublished documents. That was really the painstaking part. But I didn't want the book to be based just on people's memories."

Q: Some of the articles you cited were shocking, such as when Philadelphia magazine used the term "faggoty fags" to describe the men at the city's first Gay Pride march in 1972.
A: "I think what Philadelphia's magazines and newspapers wrote was pretty typical of the coverage nationwide. But some of The Philadelphia Tribune's coverage of early gay civil rights activism was somewhat sympathetic, which was unique for a large black city newspaper."

Q: You were a member of both ACT-UP and Queer Nation. Could you talk a little about the early days of gay activism?
A: "In Philadelphia it really started in 1960 with mimeographed newsletters, and then Drum magazine was launched in '64. Most gay magazines before the 1950s tended to be very respectable text-heavy publications. Drum magazine for the first time brought together hard-hitting news of interest to gays and lesbians--and sexual imagery. It was the widest- circulating gay-movement magazine in the country."

Q: You have a section on Rizzo's Raiders, particularly the raid on the Humoresque coffee shop. What do you think Frank Rizzo's impact was on the gay community?
A: "I think it was a mixed record. On the one hand, police repression was extraordinary. There are accounts in the book of violence committed by the police under Rizzo's watch against gay men and lesbians, including the raid you mentioned and the crackdown on the Halloween-night tradition when drag queens paraded in and out of bars. So there are definitely repressive elements. But at the same time, because Rizzo had a relationship with organized crime and because both the police and organized crime could profit from the existence of vice bars, a vice district and gay bars, there was also a certain amount of ghettoized tolerance, and that was why there was quite a lively gay scene centered around 13th and Locust. As long as it didn't erupt too much into the streets and as long as you stayed in the neighborhood, there was a certain amount of tolerance for the gay scene."

Q: So is it fair to say that if gays and lesbians stuck to the Gayborhood, the police left them alone?
A: "I'm not sure about that. Most everyone I talked to had been arrested or knew someone who'd been arrested. So what does it mean when you're arrested one out of the 10 times you go out? It means most of the time you're fine, but one out of 10 is pretty bad if you're going out most weekends."

Q: Gay and lesbian relationships are featured prominently in the book. How did these relationships compare with straight relationships at the time?
A: "At the outset of my project I was hoping the relationships between gay men and lesbians historically would be very different than what we find among the straight community. I think in the end what I found is that relationships between lesbians and gay men are just as bad as the relationships between straight women and men. There was instance after instance of sexism among gay men, and certainly many instances of segregation."

Q: Did Philadelphia gay and lesbian African-Americans play a larger role in the queer community than they did in other cities?
A: "I think they did. Compared to New York and San Francisco, Philadelphia has a much larger percentage of African-Americans. Just from the demographics there was bound to be a greater level of black queer activity. There's evidence of that in the significant number of bars that cater to black men or a multiracial clientele, as well as the black Halloween drag-queen parade that happened on South Street. Particularly after Stonewall, Latinos, Asians and African-Americans began to play a leading role in the homophile movement."
