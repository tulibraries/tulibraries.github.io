Rookies of the Year
Bob Bloss

Bob Bloss' latest book honors baseball 'Rookies of the Year'

By M. English for The Times Herald, 31 May 2005

First, there was Jackie Robinson. That was 1947. Then, there were luminaries like Willie Mays, Cal Ripken Jr. and Derek Jeter. But whether or not their names became synonymous with America's Pastime, baseball's Rookies of the Year are special.

"This prestigious award is unique because a player has only one opportunity to win it," observes Plymouth Meeting author Bob Bloss in "Rookies of the Year," just published by Temple University Press. "Cy Young awards, Manager of the Year, and MVP honors can be achieved multiple times, as can individual 'best' years in all offensive and defensive categories."

Adds Bloss as he prepares for a book-signing session at Barnes & Noble's Metroplex store in Plymouth Meeting Saturday, "Rookie of the Year is unlike any other statistical category because it's one and done. I think that's a large part of the reason it is such an honor. It's interesting, too, when you realize how many of the big names - people like Mickey Mantle, Hank Aaron, Sandy Koufax, Mike Schmidt - were not Rookies of the Year."

Bloss's signing engagement follows guest spots on radio stations around the country - most recently for Radio KCCA in California and Fred Wallin's nationall syndicated Sports Biz program. A May 17 appearance at the Trover book store in Washington, D.C., included a reception sponsored by the D.C. Baseball Association.

A former Times Herald sports writer and seasoned freelance journalist, Bloss made the literary rounds with his first book—"Baseball Managers: Stats, Stories, and Strategies"—also published by Temple University Press, in 1999.

He interviewed several of his "Rookies of the Year" sources during games in Philadelphia, New York and Baltimore and tracked down others during spring training. All told, he talked to 40 of the 116 players profiled and nearly as many coaches, managers and other sources "closely associated" with his subjects.

Bloss remembers his conversation with Frank Robinson of the Cincinnati Reds, 1956 National League Rookie of the Year, as one of his favorites. He points to the following comments made by Robinson as the two chatted in the manager's office at the former Veterans Stadium: "After the season there was no big deal. The league phoned to say I was Rookie of the Year. That was it. No citation or anything. But there was a fan club in Cincinnati—some people who got to know me that summer. They chipped in, bought a trophy with all the statistics and so forth, and presented it to me at an informal neighborhood get-together. I still cherish that trophy. It's the only one I got that signifies Rookie of the Year."

Recalls Bloss, "I'd been told beforehand that Frank Robinson was something of a curmudgeon, but he couldn't have been nicer. I found, though, that virtually everybody I talked to was pleased to reminisce about their first year, whether it was one year ago or 45 years ago. "For example, Mark McGwire said, 'All anybody wants to talk about is...home runs and...the long ball. Thanks for letting me talk about something else.' "

In addition to his consideration of each Rookie of the Year, Bloss addresses trends within teams. For instance:

"The Los Angeles Dodgers had an unprecedented five Rookies of the Year in a row, while the Pittsburgh Pirates had none until last year.

"Of the 29 pitchers who earned Rookies of the Year laurels, only one was a 20-game winner in his Rookie season.

"Only two teams— the expansion Arizona and Tampa Bay clubs—have failed to produce a Rookie of the Year."

Bloss' personal passion for sports began when he was a kid playing pick-up ball with neighborhood pals in Erie and attending high school football and basketball games with his father. Apparently, he had no illusions about his own chances for Rookie of the Year status. "(I am) a former second-string high school outfielder who could hit a curve ball only when (I) knew it was coming...and, then, not very far," he quips.

At Temple University, Bloss did announcing for campus radio, served as yearbook sports editor and earned a communications degree in 1957. He went on to announcer jobs for radio stations in Erie and Wilmington, Del., and WHYY-TV/FM in Philadelphia. He also did sports play-by-play for WNPV, WNAR, WIFI and WXTU.

Following his stint at The Times Herald, Bloss spent 24 years as national merchandising manager and advertiser special events director for TV Guide Magazine. He specializes in articles related to baseball—including 10 years' worth of columns for the Phillies Report and feature stories for Temple Review—but is also a regular feature contributor to Rough Notes Magazine, a national insurance monthly. Bloss belongs to the Philadelphia Sports Writers Association, the Society for American Baseball Research, the Philadelphia Athletics Historical Society and the Montgomery County Coaches Hall of Fame advisory board. He and wife Floriana have two daughters and two grandchildren.

Rookies of the Year, illustrated with some 50 player photos and 119 tables, retails for $19.95 and is also available online at amazon.com.
